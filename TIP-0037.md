## Abstract

"This proposal explores the implications of the Landauer limit in computer security, equating energy requirements for secure operations with mass equivalents. By leveraging the relationship between information theory and thermodynamics, it aims to establish a foundational framework for security specifications in various computational environments."

## Motivation

"Understanding the energy constraints associated with information processing is critical in developing secure systems. This exploration of 'mass of a bit' assists in defining measurable security standards, enhancing robustness against emerging threats."

## Specification

"The specifications outlined need to establish security metrics quantifiable in terms of energy and mass. This includes secure erasure requirements, power-analysis resistance parameters, tamper-evidence thresholds, and temperature-controlled security practices."

## Rationale

"By assessing security through a physical lens (i.e., the mass-energy perspective), system designers can better anticipate vulnerabilities and design more resilient systems. This framework promotes interdisciplinary approaches combining physics, information theory, and computer security."

## Security Considerations

"Issues of thermal management, energy consumption, and operational environments must be critically evaluated. These considerations are vital for real-world applications, especially in environments where well-defined security thresholds are required for trust."

## Implementation

"Implementation of this framework will require collaboration across disciplines, including material science, computer engineering, and cybersecurity. Pilot projects and testing are necessary to validate the proposed specifications in practical environments."
